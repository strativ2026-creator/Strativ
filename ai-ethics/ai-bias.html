AI Bias and Fairness

What is Bias in AI?

Bias in artificial intelligence refers to systematic errors that produce unfair outcomes, often favoring certain groups over others. It can be understood through three primary lenses.

Statistical Perspective

Bias is measured as deviation between model predictions and actual ground truth. It focuses on accuracy gaps and error distributions across groups — quantifying how much a system's outputs differ from reality for different populations.

Social Perspective

Bias manifests as systematic disadvantage imposed on certain groups based on protected characteristics such as race, gender, or socioeconomic status. It is rooted in historical inequities and reflects societal power dynamics embedded in data.

Ethical Perspective

Bias violates moral principles such as justice, dignity, or equal respect. This perspective is normative — concerned with what ought to be rather than pure measurement — and asks whether an AI outcome is morally acceptable.

What is Fairness in AI?

Fairness is the absence of prejudice or favoritism toward individuals or groups based on intrinsic or acquired characteristics. It operates across three dimensions.

Formal Fairness

Consistent application of rules; equal treatment before the law. Every individual is assessed by the same criteria regardless of group membership.

Substantive Fairness

Equitable distribution of opportunities and outcomes; addressing structural inequalities. Recognises that identical treatment of unequal groups may perpetuate rather than resolve injustice.

Procedural Fairness

Transparency and accountability in decision-making processes. Individuals are entitled to understand how decisions affecting them are reached and to contest those decisions.

Common Sources of Bias in Machine Learning

Bias enters AI systems at every stage of the development pipeline — from how data is collected to how models are designed and ultimately deployed in the real world.

Data-Level Biases
Historical Bias

Pre-existing societal discrimination encoded in data. Example: COMPAS reflected racial disparities in arrests because it trained on historically biased arrest records.

Representation Bias

Underrepresentation of certain groups. Example: ImageNet datasets are dominated by images from Western and Northern hemisphere countries.

Measurement Bias

Systematic errors in how features are measured. Example: Using arrest records as a proxy for "criminality" over-represents minority groups who are disproportionately policed.

Sampling Bias

Non-random selection of training data. Example: Online surveys exclude populations without internet access.

Labeling / Annotation Bias

Human annotator prejudices reflected in ground-truth labels. Example: African American English is disproportionately labelled as offensive in hate-speech datasets.

Algorithm-Level Biases
Algorithmic Bias

Model architecture amplifies existing biases. Example: Deep neural networks learn spurious correlations that reinforce stereotypes.

Aggregation Bias

A "one-size-fits-all" model ignores subgroup differences. Example: A healthcare model performs well overall but poorly for minority ethnic groups.

Evaluation Bias

Using inappropriate benchmarks. Example: Facial recognition benchmarks lacked dark-skinned subjects, giving inflated performance claims.

Deployment-Level Biases
Interaction Bias

User behaviour shapes algorithmic feedback loops. Example: Search engines reinforce popularity bias because users click more on top results.

Emergent Bias

Bias arising in unanticipated contexts. Example: A COVID-19 symptom checker fails for non-English speakers.

Automation Bias

Overreliance on AI outputs. Example: Doctors deferring to faulty diagnostic models even when clinical signs contradict them.

Frameworks and Metrics for Measuring Fairness

Researchers have proposed a wide range of mathematical fairness criteria. Each captures a different moral intuition — and they often conflict with one another, reflecting the genuine complexity of the concept.

Group Fairness Metrics

Demographic / Statistical Parity The proportion of positive outcomes (e.g., hired, admitted) is equal across groups. Application: Hiring, admissions, loan approvals.
Equalized Odds Both false-positive and true-positive rates are equal across groups. Application: Criminal justice, medical diagnosis.
Equal Opportunity True-positive rates are equal across groups — focuses on correctly identifying qualified individuals. Application: Scholarships, targeted interventions.
Predictive Parity Positive predictive value is equal across groups — a positive prediction is equally likely to be correct for all groups. Application: Risk assessment, credit scoring.

Individual Fairness Metrics

Fairness Through Awareness Similar individuals should receive similar outcomes, using an explicitly defined similarity metric.
Distance-Based Fairness The distance between predictions should not exceed the distance between individuals' features (Lipschitz condition).
Ranking-Based Fairness The ranking of predicted outcomes should align with the ranking of individual similarities.

Causal Fairness Metrics

Counterfactual Fairness A decision is fair if it would be the same had the sensitive attribute been different in a counterfactual world.
Path-Specific Fairness Only allowable causal paths may influence decisions; discriminatory pathways are blocked.
Total Fairness No direct or indirect discrimination via any causal pathway.

Key Trade-offs

Incompatibility: Kleinberg et al. (2016) proved that equalized odds and calibration cannot be simultaneously satisfied except in trivial cases — enforcing one metric mathematically violates another.

Fairness–Accuracy Trade-off: Optimizing for fairness often reduces overall predictive accuracy, forcing designers to make explicit value judgements about which errors are most harmful.

Individual vs. Group Fairness: Achieving group-level fairness may require treating similar individuals differently; achieving individual fairness may preserve group-level disparities.

Real-World Bias and Fairness Challenges

The following case studies show how AI bias causes measurable harm in high-stakes domains — from criminal justice to employment and healthcare.

COMPAS Recidivism Prediction

ContextRisk assessment tool used in U.S. courts for pretrial and sentencing decisions.
FindingAfrican American defendants were twice as likely to be falsely flagged as high-risk; white defendants were more likely to be falsely flagged as low-risk (ProPublica, 2016).
Technical issueThe model achieved calibration but violated equalized odds — a fairness impossibility in practice.
ImpactPerpetuated racial disparities in incarceration and bail decisions.

Healthcare Algorithm (Obermeyer et al., 2019)

ContextAlgorithm predicting healthcare needs for approximately 200 million Americans.
FindingBlack patients were significantly sicker than white patients at the same risk score.
CauseUsed healthcare spending as a proxy for health need — Black patients have lower spending due to systemic barriers to care.
ImpactDenied needed care to Black patients; correcting the bias would make the model 50% more accurate.

Automated Hiring — Amazon (2018)

ContextAI recruiting tool built to screen candidates for technical roles.
FindingSystem penalised resumes containing the word "women's" and downgraded graduates of all-women's colleges.
CauseTrained on 10 years of predominantly male applicant data, encoding existing gender imbalance as a learned preference.
ImpactSystematic gender discrimination; the tool was scrapped before deployment.

ESL Students in Automated Essay Scoring

ContextAutomated grading systems deployed in e-learning platforms globally.
FindingEnglish as a Second Language (ESL) students consistently received lower scores than native speakers of equivalent ability.
CauseTraining data dominated by native-English essays, so non-native writing patterns were penalised as errors.
ImpactUnfair assessment of linguistic ability; hindered educational progression for non-native English speakers.

Ethical and Social Implications of Biased AI

Bias in AI is not merely a technical glitch — it has profound consequences for discrimination, equality, institutional trust, and fundamental human rights.

Discrimination

Direct discrimination — Explicit use of protected attributes (race, gender) leads to unfavourable outcomes.
Indirect discrimination — Neutral proxies (e.g., zip code, name) correlate with protected attributes and produce discriminatory effects.
Proxy discrimination — Race inferred from geography, language, or purchasing patterns without ever naming race explicitly.
Intersectional discrimination — Bias at the intersection of multiple identities (e.g., dark-skinned women) is frequently overlooked by single-axis fairness metrics.

Social Inequality

Economic concentration — AI benefits accrue to a few tech companies; labour displacement affects vulnerable populations most.
Digital divide — AI tools remain inaccessible to communities lacking infrastructure, compounding existing inequalities.
Data exclusion — Underrepresented groups become invisible to AI systems, perpetuating their marginalisation in future models.
Amplification loops — Biased outcomes feed back into training data, reinforcing and strengthening future biases over time.

Trust and Accountability

Problem of many hands — Responsibility is diffused across developers, deployers, and users, making clear attribution of harm difficult.
Opacity — Black-box models prevent meaningful accountability; neither users nor regulators can audit what they cannot see.
Ethics washing — Using voluntary principles and guidelines to preempt binding regulation while maintaining harmful practices.
Liability gaps — Existing legal frameworks are ill-equipped to assign responsibility for harms caused by complex AI systems.

Human Rights Violations

Privacy — AI-enabled surveillance and re-identification threaten individuals' informational privacy and autonomy.
Freedom of expression — Content moderation algorithms can disproportionately silence minority voices and non-dominant languages.
Due process — Algorithmic decisions often lack meaningful explanation or accessible mechanisms for contestation.
Right to non-discrimination — Protected under international law (UDHR Art. 7, ICCPR Art. 2, EU Charter Art. 21); biased AI may violate these obligations.
