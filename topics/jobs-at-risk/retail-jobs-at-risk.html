Retail Jobs at Risk

Retail stands as one of the industries most profoundly transformed by AI and automation. Unlike abstract future scenarios, the disruption is happening now—visible in every shopping mall and big-box store across the globe. The changes are reshaping both how retail operates and who works in it.

The Scale of Retail Automation Risk

65% of retail jobs face automation by 2025 due to technological advances and cost pressures. This is largely due to technological advancements, rising costs and wages, tight labor markets, and reduced consumer spending.

To put this in perspective: Retail accounts for 40% of industries most affected by AI-driven automation, making it second only to manufacturing in terms of transformation scope.

Cashiers: The Hardest Hit

The most visible transformation is occurring in checkout and point-of-sale positions. Self-checkout systems, mobile payment apps and cashier-less stores like Amazon Go are redefining retail checkout. Human cashiers are increasingly being replaced by automated systems that streamline the entire point-of-sale process. Today, AI has the ability to take care of every part of the transaction, from item detection to payment processing. In London alone, AI is set to displace up to 26,000 cashiers.

Specific company implementations show the scale: Walmart's self-checkout expansion could replace 8,000 positions, while Sam's Club's AI verification rollout is projected to eliminate 12,000 cashier jobs across its stores.

The Technological Drivers

Multiple AI technologies are converging to automate retail work. Retail and customer service face the most direct AI impact on front-line workers. Automation creates opportunities driven by self-checkout systems, automated inventory management, and AI-powered recommendation engines.

From self-checkout machines to AI-powered booking systems, automation is changing how people shop and travel. Grocery stores, airports, and hotels are integrating AI to manage everything from inventory to guest services. Also in hotels, AI concierge bots, facial recognition check-in, and robot-cleaners are being tested to offset labor shortages and rising operational costs.

The Retail Jobs Most at Risk

  1. Cashiers & Checkout Workers (Highest Risk) - The most obvious displacement, with self-checkout now accounting for significant retail transactions. These roles are being eliminated fastest across the industry.
  2. Stock & Inventory Staff - Retailers are using agentic AI to drive inventory optimization, shelf management, checkout automation and predictive customer experiences. Automated inventory systems are reducing the need for manual stock management.
  3. Customer Service Representatives - Chatbots and AI-powered customer service systems handle routine inquiries and complaints faster than human representatives.
  4. Retail Supervisors & Floor Managers - Lower-level supervisory roles are being consolidated as stores operate with leaner management structures supported by AI analytics.
  5. Warehouse & Distribution Center Workers - Automation in receiving, sorting, and shipping operations is eliminating positions in retail supply chains.
  6. Sales Associates in Routine Roles - Associates handling basic transactions, restocking, and routine customer inquiries are increasingly replaced by digital systems.

Who's Most Vulnerable in Retail

The impact is not equally distributed across the workforce:

  • Age Factor: Young workers (22–25 years) are most affected, with employment in AI-exposed roles falling 6–20%, while older workers often see stable or growing employment.
  • Gender Disparity: In the U.S., 79% of employed women work in jobs that are at high risk of automation, compared to 58% of men. In high-income countries, jobs most vulnerable to AI-driven task automation make up 9.6% of female employment, which is almost three times the proportion for male jobs (3.2%).
  • Skill Level: Entry-level and less-educated workers in retail face the steepest displacement risk, as their positions are most vulnerable to direct automation.

Workforce Transformation Challenges

Workforce transformation will become a defining factor in retail, with IDC projecting that by 2028, half of major retailers will deploy advanced tools to close the digital and AI skills gap. It is still unclear what the actual digital and AI skills needed to successfully navigate retail roles as a store associate, store manager or corporate retailer are. It makes more sense to bring people on board who can learn and adapt quickly and arm them with trust than to manage the tech and force employees to conform.

IDC projects that by 2027, 80% of agentic AI use cases will require real-time, contextual data access pushing retailers toward federated data architectures for greater agility and compliance.

The Retail Turnover Problem

Even before AI acceleration, retail faced significant workforce challenges. The retail sector's 24.9% turnover rate indicates ongoing workforce instability. But AI tools that reduce repetitive tasks and provide better customer insights may improve job satisfaction for workers who embrace these technologies.

Emerging Opportunities in Retail

Despite the displacement, new roles are emerging within transformed retail environments. AI-focused retail roles include: AI inventory management specialists, data analysts interpreting customer behavior, customer experience managers (high-touch relationships), digital transformation coordinators, and omnichannel experience designers.

This automation creates opportunities for workers who can manage AI systems, analyze customer data, and provide personalized service that AI cannot replicate.

Transition Pathways for At-Risk Retail Workers

The Path Forward: Upskilling early helps to "transition more smoothly and confidently into new roles." New roles should focus on specialized support areas, like technical troubleshooting, product training or client onboarding — areas where empathy and product expertise are harder to automate. Workers in these environments bring a valuable understanding of consumer behavior to the table. Digital literacy and adaptability are key to remaining competitive in the retail space.

Critical skills to develop:

  • Customer relationship management
  • Data analytics and interpretation
  • Omnichannel experience design
  • Advanced customer service (for complex issues)
  • Inventory and supply chain analysis
  • Digital tool proficiency

The Companies Leading the Charge

Major retailers are accelerating automation: Amazon Go with fully automated cashier-less stores, Walmart with aggressive self-checkout expansion, Sam's Club with AI verification rollout, and various grocers where self-checkout now accounts for significant transaction volume.

The Critical Retention Issue

The transformation emphasizes human-AI collaboration. But here's what the headlines miss: While AI eliminates some roles, it's creating far more new ones for workers who embrace these technologies and develop the right skills.

Geographic Variation

The impact varies by location:

  • Urban centers: Higher automation adoption due to technology access and higher labor costs
  • Suburban areas: Mixed adoption depending on company policy and local circumstances
  • Rural areas: Slower automation due to lower labor replacement incentives and infrastructure challenges

The Paradox: Efficiency vs. Employment

Convenience: Self-checkout kiosks can serve more customers in less time without waiting for a cashier. Personalization: AI uses customer data to make tailored recommendations, increasing conversion rates.

Yet 41% of companies plan workforce cuts due to AI by 2030. The retail sector is caught between the need for efficiency and the consequences of workforce reduction. Companies that invest in retraining existing workers—like Amazon's $700 million commitment—position themselves better for long-term sustainability and brand reputation.

The Bottom Line for Retail Workers

Retail employment is undergoing fundamental transformation. The traditional pathway—start as a cashier, advance to supervisor, potentially to management—is disappearing for many. Instead, retail workers who survive and thrive will need to:

  • Embrace technology - Learn to work with AI systems, not against them
  • Develop soft skills - Emphasize emotional intelligence and customer relationships
  • Gain analytical capability - Understand data, customer behavior, inventory systems
  • Build adaptability - Be ready to transition multiple times throughout their careers
  • Pursue upskilling early - Don't wait for displacement to start learning

By 2028, half of major retailers will deploy advanced tools to close the digital and AI skills gap. The window for proactive upskilling is closing—retail workers who haven't started developing AI-adjacent skills are falling behind rapidly.
